About Me

I am a Research Scientist at CSIRO’s Data61 in Melbourne, Australia, where I develop autonomous, adaptive cybersecurity systems to defend against evolving threats and protect critical infrastructure. My research combines Artificial Intelligence, Game Theory, and Graph Data Mining to build security solutions that can anticipate, detect, and respond to attacks in real time. By integrating machine learning, multi-agent systems, and graph analytics, I design defense architectures that are not only technically robust but also scalable, resilient, and aligned with real-world operational demands. My expertise spans graph-based anomaly detection, reinforcement learning for cyber threat mitigation, and strategic reasoning under uncertainty. I’m particularly focused on advancing security systems that move beyond reactive response—towards self-adaptive, intelligent defense mechanisms that can evolve with the threat landscape.

Prior to my current role, I was a Postdoctoral Fellow at CSIRO’s Data61 in collaboration with the Cyber Security Cooperative Research Centre (CSCRC), where I contributed to national cyber defense initiatives, delivering research-driven solutions for government, infrastructure operators, and industry partners.

Research

My research focuses on designing strategic, AI-driven cybersecurity architectures that are autonomous, scalable, and resilient—targeted at securing complex, dynamic, and adversarial digital environments.

Autonomous Cyber Defense

I develop intelligent defense systems capable of real-time, autonomous decision-making in high-stakes security environments, where rapid response and adaptability are critical.

  • Large Language Models (LLMs) for context-aware threat detection and automated reasoning
  • Deep reinforcement learning for adaptive intrusion mitigation and evolving policy strategies
  • Continual learning pipelines that adapt to novel threats without retraining
  • AI integration with zero-trust architectures and real-time incident response systems

These systems are designed to operate at scale, reduce human workload, and proactively evolve with the threat landscape.

Graph-Based Network Security

I use graph-based methods to model and secure large-scale, interconnected systems, leveraging the structural richness of graphs to uncover hidden threats and dependencies.

  • Graph neural networks (GNNs) for scalable intrusion and anomaly detection
  • Graph combinatorial optimization for attack surface minimization and network hardening
  • Modeling of communication topologies, control flows, and dependency graphs in distributed systems
  • Applications across critical infrastructure, IoT ecosystems, and enterprise networks

These techniques allow for robust threat detection in real-world environments where systems are large, distributed, and inherently dynamic.

Game-Theoretic Cybersecurity

Understanding the strategic behavior of adversaries is key to effective defense. I apply game theory to model attacker-defender interactions and optimize defensive strategies under uncertainty.

  • Stackelberg and evolutionary games for proactive, anticipatory defense
  • Bayesian and partially observable games for reasoning under incomplete information
  • Multi-agent reinforcement learning for decentralized and cooperative defense coordination
  • Strategic use of deception, deterrence, and misinformation to disrupt adversarial planning

Game-theoretic approaches enable defenders to think ahead, allocate resources efficiently, and manage cyber risk under adversarial pressure.
